Who Were the First Cowboys?

The real history of the American cowboy looks nothing like Hollywood. Explore the Indigenous, Mexican, and Black origins of cowboy culture.

The myth vs. the reality

For generations, popular culture has portrayed the American cowboy as a white, Anglo man riding the open range. The historical record tells a very different story.

Origin

The vaquero tradition

Hundreds of years before the American cowboy, Indigenous Mesoamerican men trained by Spanish settlers were herding cattle across what is now Mexico, Texas, Arizona, and California.

Erasure

Written out of history

Dime store novels, Wild West shows, and Hollywood films systematically replaced the real, multicultural cowboy with a romanticized white hero — a process historians have called deliberate mythologization.

Reality

A multicultural workforce

The cattle industry was built by Indigenous people, Mexican vaqueros, enslaved Black people, and freed Black men working together on ranches and trail drives across the American West.

"All of the skills, traditions, and ways of working with cattle are very much rooted in the Mexican vaquero. If you are a cowboy in the U.S. today, you have developed what you know from the vaquero."

— Kendall Nelson, photographer and cowboy historian

The vaquero tradition

The word "vaquero" comes from the Spanish word vaca, meaning cow. These were the world's first cowboys — skilled, proud, and largely forgotten by mainstream history.

Who they were

Indigenous and mestizo men

The original vaqueros were largely Indigenous Mesoamerican men trained by Spanish missionaries beginning in 1519. Over time, mestizos — men of mixed Spanish and Indigenous heritage — joined their ranks.

What they invented

The tools of cowboy culture

Vaqueros invented or perfected the lasso, chaps, the western saddle, and the techniques of horse taming and cattle roping. Every tool associated with the American cowboy traces directly to vaquero culture.

Their reach

From Mexico to California

As Spain expanded its empire westward, vaqueros spread from central Mexico through Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, and into California, establishing ranching cultures that Anglo settlers would later inherit.

"It's a forgotten history of centuries of horsemanship in the Americas that root the vaqueros to the colonial past."

— Pablo A. Rangel, independent historian

The caballero vs. the vaquero

A class distinction

A caballero was a gentleman on horseback — the wealthy ranch owner. A vaquero was a worker on horseback who did the actual cattle work. The caballero owned the land; the vaquero worked it. Despite their lower social status, vaqueros were extraordinarily skilled and deeply proud of their craft.

Black cowboys

Enslaved Black people in Texas were doing skilled cowboy work as early as the 1820s and 1830s — decades before the Civil War and long before the image of the cowboy entered popular culture.

Before emancipation

Enslaved cowboys

Enslaved Black people on Texas ranches and plantations were trained in cattle herding, horse breaking, and roping — often learning from Mexican vaqueros. They were doing skilled cowboy work long before the Civil War ended.

After emancipation

Free Black cowboys

Following the Civil War, thousands of formerly enslaved people continued working as cowboys. The West offered far greater opportunities for dignity and livelihoods than the post-Reconstruction South.

Their erasure

Written out of the story

Publishers, film studios, and dime novel writers systematically excluded Black cowboys from their stories to appeal to white audiences — a process that continued well into the 20th century.

A timeline of cowboy origins

The history of the cowboy spans five centuries and three continents.

1519

Spanish arrive in Mexico

Spanish conquistadors arrive in what is now Mexico, bringing cattle and horses. They begin training Indigenous Mesoamerican men to herd cattle on horseback — the birth of the vaquero tradition.

1598

Don Juan de Oñate's expedition

One of the richest men in New Spain leads an expedition across the Rio Grande into present-day New Mexico, bringing approximately 7,000 animals. Vaqueros have now been perfecting their craft for nearly 80 years.

1821

Anglo settlers arrive in Texas

Led by Stephen F. Austin, Anglo settlers arrive in Texas and encounter millions of longhorn cattle roaming free — cattle the vaqueros had been managing for over 200 years. Many bring enslaved people with them.

1830s–1860s

Enslaved Black cowboys in Texas

Enslaved Black people on Texas ranches and plantations are trained as cowboys. By 1860 Texas has over 2.76 million cattle — requiring thousands of cowboys, many of them enslaved.

1865

Emancipation and the cattle drives

The Civil War ends. Thousands of formerly enslaved Black cowboys continue their work, riding the great cattle trails north from Texas. Historians estimate up to 25% of all trail cowboys were African American.

1870s–1880s

The heyday of the cattle drives

The height of the open range cattle era. Cowboys of all backgrounds ride the Chisholm, Goodnight-Loving, and other major trails. One in three cowboys is a Mexican vaquero.

1880s–1900s

The myth takes shape

Barbed wire ends the open range era. Dime novels and Wild West shows begin constructing the myth of the white Anglo cowboy, systematically erasing the Black and Mexican cowboys who built the industry.

20th century

Hollywood cements the myth

Film and television complete the erasure. The American cowboy becomes a white icon of nationalism and individualism — a mythology built on centuries of forgotten Black and Mexican labor.

Notable figures

Real cowboys whose stories were buried by popular mythology.

NL

Nat Love

Born enslaved, Tennessee, 1854

Headed west at age 14 after emancipation. Became one of the most celebrated Black cowboys on the frontier, working cattle drives across Texas, Arizona, and the Great Plains.

BI

Bose Ikard

Born enslaved, Mississippi, 1847

Rode with Charles Goodnight on the famous Goodnight-Loving Trail. Goodnight trusted Ikard above all others, often entrusting him to carry large sums of money at the end of cattle drives.

BP

Bill Pickett

Born in Texas, 1871

Invented the rodeo event of "bulldogging." Became a world champion rodeo performer and international star, touring England, Canada, and South America.

GM

George McJunkin

Born enslaved, Texas, c. 1851

Rose from wrangler to ranch manager in New Mexico. In 1908 discovered ancient bones that led to an archaeological find proving human existence in North America 10,000 years ago.

JO

Don Juan de Oñate

New Spain, 1550–1626

Among the first cowboys in what is now the United States. Led a major expedition across the Rio Grande in 1598, bringing thousands of animals and establishing the vaquero tradition in the American Southwest.

SW

Sam Jones Washington

Born enslaved, Texas, c. 1849

Began working with cattle at age fifteen while still enslaved. His WPA testimony is among the most direct firsthand accounts of an enslaved cowboy ever recorded.
